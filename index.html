How this is calculated & what to trust

The model. Social Security averages your highest 35 years of indexed earnings (AIME), then runs it through the progressive PIA bend-point formula. Each year you work counts as a maximum-cap year (you noted ~$220K avg, which is above the cap — earnings above the cap don't raise benefits). Each year short of 35 is filled with a $0, which drags the average down. The cap value is calibrated so a full 35-year career reproduces your SSA statement's $4,257/mo at FRA.

Claim-age factors. Claiming at 62 (5 yrs before your FRA of 67) reduces the benefit ~30%; claiming at 70 adds ~24% in delayed-retirement credits. Claim years (2053 / 2058 / 2061) are taken from your SSA screenshot.

Today's vs future dollars. The PIA is first computed in today's purchasing power, then inflated forward to the claim year at your chosen rate to approximate the real (nominal) check. SSA's own "future dollars" view uses roughly 2.4–2.5%/yr.

The 2033 toggle. The 2025 Trustees Report projects the retirement trust fund reserves deplete around 2033–2034, after which incoming taxes would cover ~77–81% of scheduled benefits absent legislation. Since you'd claim in 2053, this is a real downside scenario — though Congress has historically acted before such deadlines. The toggle applies a flat ~23% reduction.

This is a planning estimate, not financial advice or an official SSA calculation. For the authoritative number, enter this same plan (income through your FIRE year, then $0) into the "future earnings" field of your my Social Security account.
